What two states don't require insurance?

New Hampshire and Virginia are the two states that do not require traditional car insurance. In New Hampshire, drivers can legally operate a vehicle without insurance if they can demonstrate financial responsibility to cover damages in an at-fault accident. Virginia previously allowed drivers to pay an uninsured motorist fee instead of carrying insurance, though Virginia eliminated that option in 2024, now requiring minimum liability coverage. New Hampshire remains the only state with no mandatory insurance requirement. Learn more about auto insurance coverage requirements.

Is there any state in the US that doesn't require car insurance?

Yes. New Hampshire is currently the only state that does not require drivers to carry auto insurance. However, New Hampshire drivers who choose to forgo insurance must still prove they can financially cover damages and injuries they cause in an at-fault accident. If they cannot meet that financial responsibility obligation, insurance may be required. Virginia eliminated its pay-in-lieu-of-insurance option in 2024, leaving New Hampshire as the sole exception.

What states do not require proof of insurance?

New Hampshire is the only state that does not require drivers to carry or show proof of auto insurance as a standard requirement. All other states, including Colorado, mandate that drivers carry minimum liability coverage and present proof when requested by law enforcement or after an accident. Even in New Hampshire, drivers who choose to carry insurance must still be able to provide proof if asked. Most states accept digital proof of insurance on a mobile device.

Do cops know if you have no insurance?

It depends. on the state and available technology, but in many cases, yes. A growing number of states have integrated real-time insurance verification databases that law enforcement can access during a traffic stop or through license plate readers. In Colorado, officers can check insurance status electronically, often before even approaching a vehicle. Even in states without instant database access, officers can verify coverage through registration records and follow-up checks after issuing a citation.

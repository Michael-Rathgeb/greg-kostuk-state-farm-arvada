What is full coverage auto insurance in Colorado?

8 Questions Answered

What is full coverage auto insurance in Colorado?

Full coverage auto insurance is a combination of liability, collision, and comprehensive coverage that provides broad financial protection for your vehicle. Liability covers damages you cause to others, collision covers damage to your car from accidents, and comprehensive covers non-collision events like theft, hail, or fire. In Colorado, where hailstorms are frequent, comprehensive coverage is especially valuable. Lenders typically require full coverage on financed or leased vehicles. What does it mean if the coverage limits are $50,000 / $100,000?

A $50,000/$100,000 split limit is a bodily injury liability coverage structure where $50,000 is the maximum paid per injured person in a single accident, and $100,000 is the maximum paid for all injured people combined in that same accident. For example, if two people are each injured and awarded $60,000, your policy would pay $50,000 to one and $50,000 to the other — stopping at the $100,000 per-accident cap.

What's considered full coverage in Colorado?

Full coverage in Colorado typically means carrying liability insurance plus both collision and comprehensive coverage. Colorado's state minimum liability requirements are 25/50/15, but full coverage goes beyond that by adding protection for your own vehicle. Collision pays for accident-related vehicle damage regardless of fault, while comprehensive covers events like theft, vandalism, weather damage, and animal strikes. Some drivers also add uninsured motorist coverage or medical payments coverage to strengthen their overall protection.

Do I need full coverage if my car is paid off?

It depends. on your vehicle's value, your savings, and your personal risk tolerance. Once a car is paid off, no lender requires full coverage, so the decision is yours. If your vehicle is newer or worth a significant amount, full coverage still makes financial sense. If the car has depreciated substantially and your annual premium approaches or exceeds a fraction of its value, dropping to liability-only may be reasonable. What are the disadvantages of full coverage insurance?

Full coverage auto insurance offers broad protection but comes with notable drawbacks worth considering.

  • **Higher premiums:** Full coverage costs significantly more than liability-only policies.
  • **Deductibles apply:** You pay out-of-pocket costs before coverage kicks in for collision or comprehensive claims.
  • **Diminishing value on older cars:** Premiums may exceed the vehicle's actual cash value over time.
  • **Potential for over-insurance:** Paying for coverage that surpasses what your car is worth.
  • **Rate increases after claims:** Filing claims can raise future premiums.

What does 50k/100K mean in insurance?

50k/100k in insurance is a split-limit bodily injury liability notation meaning $50,000 maximum coverage per person and $100,000 maximum per accident. These numbers represent the most your insurer will pay toward medical expenses, lost wages, or legal costs for others when you are at fault. This is a common coverage structure seen on auto insurance policies across Colorado and throughout the country.

What does $100 k /$ 300K /$ 100K mean?

A 100/300/100 policy is an auto insurance split-limit structure where $100,000 is the per-person bodily injury limit, $300,000 is the per-accident bodily injury limit, and the final $100,000 covers property damage liability per accident. The first two numbers address injuries to others, while the third covers damage to vehicles or property. What does coverage limit mean?

A coverage limit is the maximum dollar amount an insurance policy will pay for a covered claim or loss. Once that threshold is reached, any remaining costs become the policyholder's financial responsibility. Limits apply per person, per accident, or per policy period depending on the coverage type. Choosing appropriate limits is important — low limits may leave significant out-of-pocket exposure after a serious accident.
